Assuming constant input prices, this reflects economies of scale (at the production function level, and typically translates to falling long-run average costs).

D. “As a firm's inputs triple, its output doubles.”  
❌ This is decreasing returns to scale (output grows slower than inputs), which would generally lead to rising average costs — i.e., diseconomies of scale.

Note: While option C describes *increasing returns to scale*, which is closely related and often underlies economies of scale, it’s important to distinguish:
- Economies of scale = ↓ average cost as output ↑ (cost-based concept).
- Increasing returns to scale = output increases by a greater proportion than inputs (technology/production-function concept).

In standard economics education contexts (especially introductory or multiple-choice exams), **C is accepted as the best indicator/exemplar of economies of scale**, because increasing returns to scale is the primary technological driver of economies of scale in the long run.

✅ Correct answer: **C**
